What is biochar?

Biochar is charcoal made from organic waste through a process called pyrolysis — heating biomass in a low-oxygen environment to temperatures between 350°C and 700°C. Without oxygen the material does not combust. Instead it carbonizes into a stable, porous, charcoal-like substance that can persist in soil for hundreds to thousands of years.

This is not a new or experimental technology. Biochar has been produced and used in agriculture for centuries. Ancient Amazonian civilizations created terra preta soils using charred organic material that remain extraordinarily fertile today. The modern commercial industry applies the same principle at industrial scale.

What it does in soil

When incorporated into agricultural soil, biochar improves water retention — crops need less irrigation. It increases the soil's ability to hold nutrients like nitrogen and phosphorus rather than losing them to runoff. It raises soil pH in acidic soils and improves microbial activity. Because the carbon is chemically stable, it remains locked in soil rather than returning to the atmosphere as CO₂.

Reading the scorecard

Carbon content (%) — Higher is better. Above 70% qualifies for carbon credit programs. The most important single number.

Ash content (%) — Lower is better. Below 10% indicates high-carbon, low-contaminant biochar. High ash can indicate poor pyrolysis conditions.

Electrical conductivity (dS/m) — Lower is better for most crops. Below 1 dS/m is safe for all applications. Above 2 may stress salt-sensitive crops.

pH — Most biochar is alkaline (7.5–9). Beneficial for acidic soils. Check before applying to already-alkaline ground.
