What is biochar?

Biochar is charcoal made from organic waste through pyrolysis — heating biomass in a low-oxygen environment to 350–700°C. Without oxygen the material carbonizes into a stable, porous substance that persists in soil for hundreds to thousands of years. Ancient Amazonian civilizations created terra preta soils using charred organic material that remain extraordinarily fertile today.

What it does in soil

Biochar improves water retention, increases nutrient holding capacity, raises pH in acidic soils, and improves microbial activity. Because the carbon is chemically stable it remains locked in soil rather than returning to the atmosphere as CO₂ — making it one of the few genuinely permanent carbon sequestration approaches available to farmers today.

Reading the scorecard

Carbon content (%)
Higher is better. Above 70% qualifies for carbon credit programs. The most important single number.
pH
Most biochar is alkaline (7.5–9). Beneficial for acidic soils. Check before applying to already-alkaline ground.
Ash content (%)
Lower is better. Below 10% indicates high-carbon, low-contaminant biochar.
Electrical conductivity (dS/m)
Lower is better. Below 1 dS/m is safe for all applications. Above 2 may stress salt-sensitive crops.
Surface area (m²/g)
Higher is better for water retention. Above 200 is good, above 400 is excellent.
