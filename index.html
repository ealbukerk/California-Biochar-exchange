What is biochar?

Biochar is charcoal made from organic waste. The process is called pyrolysis — organic material such as wood chips, agricultural residue, rice husks, almond shells, or forest thinnings is heated in a low-oxygen environment to temperatures between 350°C and 700°C. Without oxygen the material does not combust. Instead it carbonizes into a stable, porous, charcoal-like substance that can persist in soil for hundreds to thousands of years.

This is not a new or experimental technology. Biochar has been produced and used in agriculture for centuries. Ancient Amazonian civilizations created terra preta soils using charred organic material that remain extraordinarily fertile today. The modern commercial biochar industry applies the same principle at industrial scale using controlled equipment.

What biochar does in soil

When incorporated into agricultural soil, biochar improves water retention — meaning crops need less irrigation. It increases the soil's ability to hold nutrients like nitrogen and phosphorus rather than losing them to runoff, reducing fertilizer requirements. It raises soil pH in acidic soils and improves microbial activity. And because the carbon in biochar is chemically stable, it remains locked in the soil rather than returning to the atmosphere as CO₂ — making it a genuine carbon sequestration tool, not just a soil amendment.

For farms and orchards facing water scarcity and rising input costs, biochar offers a meaningful improvement to water efficiency and soil health simultaneously. Anything that credibly reduces irrigation demand while improving yields gets serious attention from growers.

Why source locally

Local sourcing means shorter supply chains, lower transport costs, and a smaller carbon footprint — important for farms making sustainability claims. It also means feedstock transparency: you know exactly what your biochar was made from and where.
